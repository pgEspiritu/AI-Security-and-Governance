# ⚠️ AI Risk Assessment

Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems are increasingly used across many sectors such as:

- 💳 Credit scoring  
- 🧑‍💼 Recruitment  
- 🏥 Medical diagnostics  
- ⚡ Power distribution  

Failures in these systems can range from **minor inconveniences** to **serious societal impacts**.

Because AI systems interact with **technical, organizational, and societal factors**, managing their risks becomes complex. Their socio-technical nature means that failures may arise not only from technical errors but also from **human behavior, data issues, or governance gaps**.

Therefore, **AI governance and risk management** are essential to detect failures early and mitigate potential harm.

---

# 🎯 Understanding AI Risk

**AI risk** refers to the potential **negative consequences and uncertainties** associated with deploying and using AI systems.

Common AI risks include:

- 🔐 **Privacy risks** – misuse or exposure of personal data  
- 🛡️ **Security vulnerabilities** – exploitation by malicious actors  
- ⚖️ **Bias and fairness issues** – discriminatory decision-making  
- 🔎 **Lack of transparency** – difficulty explaining AI decisions  
- 🚨 **Safety risks** – especially in robotics or autonomous systems  

Effective AI governance requires identifying and managing these risks **throughout the entire AI lifecycle**.

---

# 📊 NIST Definition of Risk

According to the **NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)**, risk is defined as a function of:

1️⃣ **Negative impact (magnitude of harm)**  
2️⃣ **Likelihood of occurrence**

The harm caused by AI risks may affect:

- Individuals  
- Communities  
- Organizations  
- Society  
- The environment  

Interestingly, risk assessment can also reveal **positive impacts or opportunities**, not only threats.

---

# 🇪🇺 EU AI Act Risk Classification

The **EU AI Act** introduces a **risk-based approach** for regulating AI systems.

AI systems are categorized into four levels:

| Risk Level | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| 🚫 Unacceptable Risk | AI systems that pose serious threats to rights and safety |
| ⚠️ High Risk | AI used in critical sectors like healthcare or recruitment |
| ℹ️ Limited Risk | Systems requiring transparency obligations |
| ✅ Minimal Risk | Low-risk AI applications like recommendation engines |

Generative AI and **foundation models** have additional regulatory obligations, especially if they pose **systemic risks**.

# 🤝 Defining AI Trustworthiness

Before deploying an AI system, organizations must evaluate its **trustworthiness**.

Trustworthy AI systems demonstrate several key characteristics.

---

## 🌍 Benefiting People and the Planet

AI systems should promote:

- Inclusive growth  
- Sustainable development  
- Human well-being  

Developers should design AI systems that **benefit society and the environment**.

---

## ⚖️ Human-Centered Values and Fairness

AI actors must respect:

- Human rights  
- Democratic values  
- Rule of law  

AI systems should avoid **bias and discrimination**.

---

## 🔎 Transparency and Explainability

Organizations should ensure that AI systems:

- Clearly explain how decisions are made  
- Provide understandable outcomes  
- Allow stakeholders to question or challenge decisions

---

## 🛡️ Robustness, Security, Privacy, and Safety

AI systems must be:

- Secure from cyber threats  
- Reliable and traceable  
- Safe for users and society

Risk management practices should address **technical and operational risks**.

---

## 📌 Accountability

AI actors must take responsibility for:

- The proper functioning of AI systems  
- Compliance with ethical and legal standards

Accountability ensures **trust in AI technologies**.

---

# 🔄 AI Risk Management Across the AI Lifecycle

Risk management should occur **throughout the entire AI lifecycle**.

According to OECD, the lifecycle includes four major stages.

---

## 🧠 1. Design, Data, and Model Development

This stage includes:

- Planning and design  
- Data collection and processing  
- Model training and interpretation

Risks may arise from **poor data quality, biased datasets, or flawed algorithms**.

---

## 🧪 2. Verification and Validation

Models must be tested to ensure:

- Accuracy  
- Fairness  
- Compliance with regulations

Testing helps detect **errors and biases** before deployment.

---

## 🚀 3. Deployment

Once validated, AI systems are deployed in operational environments.

Governance mechanisms ensure the system **operates responsibly and securely**.

---

## 📡 4. Operation and Monitoring

After deployment, organizations must:

- Continuously monitor AI systems  
- Track performance and risks  
- Update models when necessary

AI risks **evolve over time**, making monitoring essential.

---

# 🧩 OECD AI Risk Management Framework

The OECD framework outlines four key steps in AI risk management.

---

## 🧾 1. Defining the AI System

Organizations must define:

- AI system purpose  
- Data flows  
- Processing mechanisms  
- Stakeholders involved  
- Compliance obligations

This stage may include conducting **AI Impact Assessments**.

---

## 🔍 2. Risk Assessment

This step evaluates risks based on:

- Potential harm  
- Data privacy concerns  
- Impact on individuals or society

Examples:

- High-risk systems: hiring algorithms, loan approval systems  
- Low-risk systems: entertainment applications or games

---

## 🛠️ 3. Risk Treatment

Once risks are identified, organizations must respond.

### Risk Response Determination

Possible responses include:

- Accepting the risk  
- Mitigating the risk  
- Transferring the risk

### Risk Control Implementation

Controls may include:

- Bias mitigation tools  
- Fairness auditing mechanisms  
- Security protocols

---

## 📈 4. Monitoring and Review

AI systems must be **continuously monitored** because risks can change over time.

Monitoring activities include:

- Logging data processing activities  
- Updating data privacy notices  
- Removing outdated data

---

# 📑 AI Impact Assessments (AIIA)

An **AI Impact Assessment** evaluates the potential **benefits, risks, and costs** of deploying an AI system.

It examines:

- Data quality and bias
- Algorithmic behavior
- Legal and regulatory requirements

Impact assessments help organizations:

- Improve accountability  
- Document decision-making processes  
- Increase transparency

The level of assessment depends on the **risk profile of the AI system**.

Example:

- 🎬 Movie recommendation AI → Low scrutiny  
- 💳 Loan approval AI → High scrutiny

---

# 🔎 AI Risk Assessments

AI Risk Assessments focus on **identifying and evaluating risks** associated with AI systems.

They examine:

- Transparency
- Robustness
- Accuracy
- Potential harm

These assessments often rely on **model cards** containing:

- Model description  
- Intended use  
- Limitations  
- Ethical considerations  

Risk ratings may also evaluate:

- Toxicity
- Bias
- Malicious use
- Copyright risks
- Hallucination risks
- Energy efficiency

Based on the results, organizations can decide whether to:

- ✅ Approve the model for deployment  
- 🚧 Add guardrails or controls  
- 🚫 Block the model from use

---

# 🔄 AI Risk Assessment vs AI Risk Management

| Concept | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 🔍 Risk Assessment | Identifies and analyzes potential AI risks |
| ⚙️ Risk Management | Implements strategies to address and control risks |

Risk assessment is **one component of the broader risk management process**.

---

# 🤝 AI Vendor Risk Assessments

Organizations often rely on **third-party AI vendors**.

However, these partnerships may introduce risks such as:

- Security vulnerabilities  
- Data privacy concerns  
- Operational disruptions  
- Legal or regulatory violations

Vendor risk assessments help organizations evaluate these risks before adopting AI products.

---

## 🧾 Key Risk Domains for Vendor Evaluation

Vendor AI risks can be evaluated across five domains:

1️⃣ Organization’s use case  
2️⃣ Business integration  
3️⃣ Use of confidential data  
4️⃣ Business resiliency  
5️⃣ Potential exposure

---

## 📋 Vendor Due Diligence

Vendor assessments typically involve:

- Internal stakeholder questionnaires  
- Vendor questionnaires  
- Risk analysis reports

This helps organizations establish **trustworthy vendor relationships**.

---

# 🚀 AI Readiness Assessments

AI readiness assessments evaluate whether an organization is **prepared to adopt AI responsibly**.

These assessments analyze:

- Governance frameworks  
- Technical infrastructure  
- AI capabilities  
- Organizational culture  
- Data management practices

Examples include:

- NIST AI Readiness Assessment  
- EU AI Act Readiness Assessment

The results indicate an organization's **AI maturity level**.

---

# 📊 Comparing Different AI Assessments

| Assessment Type | Purpose |
|----------------|--------|
| ⚠️ Risk Assessment | Identifies and analyzes threats and vulnerabilities |
| 🌍 Impact Assessment | Evaluates societal and individual impacts |
| 🛠️ Risk Management | Implements strategies to control and mitigate risks |
| 🤝 Vendor Risk Assessment | Evaluates risks from third-party AI vendors |
| 🚀 Readiness Assessment | Determines organizational preparedness for AI |

---

# 🧠 Key Takeaway

Effective AI governance requires organizations to:

- 🔍 Identify AI risks early  
- 📊 Assess potential impacts  
- 🛠️ Implement risk mitigation controls  
- 📡 Continuously monitor AI systems  
- 📜 Ensure compliance with global AI regulations

By implementing structured **AI risk assessment and management frameworks**, organizations can deploy AI systems that are **secure, trustworthy, and socially responsible**.
